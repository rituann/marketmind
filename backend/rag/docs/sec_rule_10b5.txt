SEC Rule 10b-5: Employment of Manipulative and Deceptive Practices
Source: Securities and Exchange Commission — 17 CFR § 240.10b-5

OVERVIEW
Rule 10b-5, promulgated under Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, is the primary anti-fraud provision governing securities trading in the United States. It prohibits any act or omission resulting in fraud or deceit in connection with the purchase or sale of any security.

PROHIBITED CONDUCT
It is unlawful for any person, directly or indirectly, by use of any means or instrumentality of interstate commerce:
(a) To employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud;
(b) To make any untrue statement of a material fact or to omit to state a material fact necessary in order to make the statements made, in the light of the circumstances under which they were made, not misleading; or
(c) To engage in any act, practice, or course of business which operates or would operate as a fraud or deceit upon any person.

INSIDER TRADING
The most common application of Rule 10b-5 is to insider trading. Trading on material, non-public information (MNPI) is a violation. Material information is any information that a reasonable investor would consider important in making an investment decision — including earnings announcements, merger discussions, regulatory approvals, and executive changes.

MATERIAL NON-PUBLIC INFORMATION (MNPI) — EXAMPLES
- Unannounced quarterly earnings significantly above or below analyst expectations
- Pending mergers, acquisitions, or divestitures not yet publicly disclosed
- FDA drug approval or rejection decisions before public announcement
- Regulatory investigations or enforcement actions not yet disclosed
- Executive departures or appointments prior to press release

TIPPERS AND TIPPEES
Liability extends beyond the original insider. A "tipper" who discloses MNPI in breach of a duty and receives a benefit is liable. A "tippee" who receives MNPI knowing it was improperly disclosed is also liable if they trade on it.

PENALTIES
Civil penalties up to three times the profit gained or loss avoided. Criminal penalties up to $5 million for individuals and $25 million for firms. Imprisonment up to 20 years for criminal violations under the Sarbanes-Oxley Act.

SAFE HARBOR — RULE 10b5-1 PLANS
An affirmative defense is available if trades were made pursuant to a pre-established, written trading plan adopted when the person was not aware of MNPI. The plan must specify: the amount, price, and date of the trade; or a formula for determining these; or delegate trading decisions to a third party without MNPI access.
